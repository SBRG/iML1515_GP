CC   -!- FUNCTION: Catalyzes the oxidation of 3-hydroxypropionaldehyde (3-
CC       HPA) to 3-hydroxypropionic acid (3-HP). It acts preferentially
CC       with NAD but can also use NADP. 3-HPA appears to be the most
CC       suitable substrate for PuuC followed by isovaleraldehyde,
CC       propionaldehyde, butyraldehyde, and valeraldehyde. It might play a
CC       role in propionate and/or acetic acid metabolisms. Also involved
CC       in the breakdown of putrescine through the oxidation of gamma-Glu-
CC       gamma-aminobutyraldehyde to gamma-Glu-gamma-aminobutyrate (gamma-
CC       Glu-GABA). {ECO:0000269|PubMed:15590624,
CC       ECO:0000269|PubMed:18668238}.
CC   -!- CATALYTIC ACTIVITY: Gamma-glutamyl-gamma-aminobutyraldehyde +
CC       NAD(+) + H(2)O = gamma-glutamyl-gamma-aminobutyrate + NADH.
CC   -!- CATALYTIC ACTIVITY: An aldehyde + NAD(P)(+) + H(2)O = a
CC       carboxylate + NAD(P)H.
CC   -!- ENZYME REGULATION: Lithium ions exhibits the highest inhibition
CC       (97%). To a lesser extent (5-20%), potassium, sodium, and ammonium
CC       ions also inhibit PuuC activity. Transition metals, such as copper
CC       and zinc ions inhibit PuuC activity by more than 90%. The presence
CC       of heavy metals (mercury, silver) or sodium hydrogensulfite in the
CC       reaction mixture completely inactivate PuuC; in contrast,
CC       disulfide reductants such as DTT and 2-mercaptoethanol
CC       significantly increase its activity by 75% and 27%, respectively.
CC       {ECO:0000269|PubMed:18668238}.

CC       pH dependence:
CC         Optimum pH is 8. Activity is highly sensitive to pH variation,
CC         and an increase or decrease of one pH unit from the optimal
CC         attenuates more than 70% of activity. At pH 6.0, only 6% of the
CC         activity remains. At pH.5.0, the activity is completely
CC         abolished. {ECO:0000269|PubMed:18668238};
CC       Temperature dependence:
CC         Optimum temperature is 37 degrees Celsius. It appears to be
CC         stable between 20 and 50 degrees Celsius but becomes very
CC         unstable at or above 50 degrees Celsius.
CC         {ECO:0000269|PubMed:18668238};
